donkey skin

by Alexis Truman

  In a faraway place filled with blue men, rhinestones, and time-traveling fairies, the surreal French musical called Donkey Skin takes place. Directed by a notable figure of the French New Wave movement, Jacques Demy, it’s a retelling of an eccentric fairytale written over three centuries ago. Demy preserves the magic of the original while staying true his own campy style.

  As the film begins, the queen of the kingdom (Catherine Deneuve) falls ill and dies. The heartbroken king (Jean Marair) professes he could never love again, but the queen has a bizarre request—she asks that he gets remarried only after finding someone more beautiful than herself, so he can create an heir to the throne. His men look for a queen, but none are to his liking until he is shown a picture of his own daughter (Catherine Deneuve). He’s ultimately talked into asking his daughter to be his wife by his advisors, and naturally, she’s appalled. She seeks advice from her fairy godmother (Delphine Seyric) whose glamor reflects that of a 1920s flapper girl. She gives the princess sound advice in the form of a song where she breaks down why children shouldn’t marry their parents. She references items from the future and constantly alludes that there was a failed romance between her and the king, leaving her scorned.

   The princess attempts to stop the incestuous marriage by giving him unreasonable requests she expects to be impossible, but when her father successfully gifts her dresses that capture the beauty of the sun and the sky, she’s forced to take extreme measures. She insists on one last gift—a skin made from the kingdom’s prized donkey which poops treasure. As the film went on I began to realize why there was never a children’s adaptation. Naturally, the king is apprehensive to slaughter the source of his riches, but he obliges, gifting her a donkey skin that she uses as a disguise to run away.

   Using the gifts of her oppressive father, she makes a new life for herself. She lives in a cottage in a faraway kingdom and is subject to ridicule from cruel townspeople. When a prince (Jacques Perrin) finds his way to her on a walk through the woods where the pair falls instantly in love. He becomes sick upon arriving home and his only desire is a cake baked by the mysterious girl known as Donkey Skin. Begrudgingly, his parents send guards to make his cake dreams come true. The princess bakes it, slipping her ring into the batter. Naturally, once finding the ring the prince invites every girl in the kingdom to try it on until finding the maiden it fits.

   The unusual tale combined with Demy’s unique storytelling makes donkey skin instantly iconic. At face value, the original story isn’t exactly special. It echoes the same rags-to-riches sentiments of Cinderella with added creepy incestuous stakes. However, the absurdity of the fairytale works together with Demy’s high-art style and references to modern society to produce a kind of dramatic irony—the fairy godmother and the audience are the only ones who truly seem to grasp how bizarre the princess’s conflict is. She likely shares the morals and sense of the general audience because she’s from the future. This is seen in her allusions to modern technology, liberated outlook, and above all else when she makes an entrance to her own wedding on a helicopter.

   Much like his previous films, the atmosphere of the movie is highly stylized, but Demy’s signature bright colors and charming musical numbers seem to serve Donkey Skin in a different way. The selectively historically accurate costumes and dreamy sets make for a film that transcends space and time. It challenges the already subversive French New Wave movement as it turns its back on realism entirely to pursue something that is ridiculous in the best way.
