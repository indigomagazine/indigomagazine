The Psychology of Fear

Story by Emma Lambert. Photos by Katheryn and Manjiri.





One commonality found within the human experience is
the frequent run-in with the overwhelming feeling of fear.
Despite this recurrent encounter, fear is far from being
completely and truthfully understood.
This normal emotional reaction is complex beyond our
comprehension. Fear is multi-leveled, multi-faceted and
requires an understanding of the intricacy of the brain.






Fear manifests itself in many ways, whether a physical
sensation or an overpowering rush of emotion that relates
back to our individual experiences. Fear encompasses four
components: sensation, feeling, cognition, and behavior.
These differing elements all work together to create the
ineffable intensity of the emotion we know as fear.


Our emotions can be more fluidly expressed in terms of
color. Joy is represented by bright sunshine yellow, and
sadness often being portrayed as the inundation of dark
blue waves, while fear is left to be interpreted by the
fearful. As the floods of fear overcome me, I often
associate it with a deep, brooding red color, which seems
to cloud my vision of anything but a focus on the crushing
burden of fear.



The vessels in which fear buries itself are crafted by the
fearful themselves. The range of ways fear is able to
disguise itself is infinite. Disparities between individuals
are what allow for the seemingly endless growth of fear. A
suffocating, and constant reminder of the inability to
escape the cage placed around you by the mind itself.
Fear is what materializes from our own hidden anxieties.


Fear possesses the ability to expose the most human parts
of our minds. It unearths the pains and terrors we often
push into the barren crevices found within ourselves. The
vulnerability fear creates is what allows us to fully release
into the arms of another. The shattering emotion of fear is
truthfully, the most natural and mortal way that our minds
can express themselves. There is nothing more human than
our ability to experience true fear.
