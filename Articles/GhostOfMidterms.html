ghost of midterms

Story by Amber Photo by Bansari

It is said that during the month of October one can hear phantom echoes of a weeping chorus in the halls of university campuses that sends shivers down students' spines. If you pause your studies for just a moment, you will hear it, too.

“I have to go summon a demon,” Darcy said, “so you can go on ahead. I’ll catch up.”
Nicole snorted and rolled their eyes, sliding their lunch bag to their wrist as they opened their burgundy umbrella. “Yeah, right. Catch you in a bit.”
The purple circle of Nicole’s umbrella disappeared into the colorful mass of umbrellas as the crowd of university students spilled out from their morning classes. Darcy turned from the door and started down the hall. The dense fog from that morning had disappeared, replaced by rain. She peered out the large gothic windows as she walked the halls, watching the steady droplets turn into an oppressive sheet of gray.
It wasn’t long before Darcy stood at the door of a stairwell tucked far away from the hustle and bustle of the main campus. She tested the handle. It was unlocked.
Letting out a shaky breath, she opened the door to pitch black. She fumbled for her phone and turned the flashlight on. The light shone on wooden stairs that Darcy could bet were never made to hold even a feather, let alone a human being. The legend said that the seeker of the demon would find them at the thirteenth step, so that’s what she did.
She counted in her head—one, two, three, four—until she reached step thirteen, at which point her eyes darted to her surroundings waiting for a scary demon to pop out of nowhere.
Honestly, Darcy couldn’t believe she was doing this all for good grades on her midterms, but she was desperate at this point; she’d almost give her left kidney to make As. For her sake, she was glad she didn’t have to part with any of her organs (she would much rather keep them if she could, thank you very much), but she valued her GPA more than anything, especially while attending a prestigious university.

A popping noise below her sent a shiver up her spine. She shut her eyes, waiting for the inevitable—it was too late to turn back now.
She heard someone clear their throat and she squeezed her eyes tighter. Footsteps were coming near her. She almost stopped breathing.
“Are you going to open your eyes?” A deep, buttery voice said. “I promise I am not ugly in the slightest.”
The unkown person tilted her chin up. Cautiously, she opened her eyes. They dropped their hand from her chin and stepped back.
“In fact,” they said, tilting their head to the side, “I’m probably the prettiest thing you’ll have ever seen.”
Darcy could admit they were quite beautiful. Long, black lashes lined their small eyes, their thick eyebrows to die for, and their lips had a rosy tint that Darcy could tell was natural. Their outfit, however, was a collage of different aesthetics and styles haphazardly thrown together. The floral scarf tied on their head, the green polyester shirt, and the flowy floor-length skirt made them look like someone’s grandma.
She frowned. “I never thought a demon could look so… tacky.”
Their bushy eyebrows shot to the sky. “One, I am not a demon, I am a ghost,” they said, defiantly wagging their finger.
“…Isn’t that the same thing?”
“No, not in the slightest! And two–” they stood at their fullest height, shoulders rolled back, head raised high “–do you think a demon could be so handsome?”
“Yes,” they said, as they fake-sobbed into their hands. “Actually that would be the most logical answer.”

They let out another dramatically loud sob, then looked at her with a contemptuous glare, though Darcy could see their lip quivering.
“I am the ghost of midterms past. It is an insult to my ancestors to be called a demon.” They whipped their head to the side, placing their hand on their chest. “For you to say that in such a sacred place as this…”
For the first time, Darcy took in the changed environment, her frown deepening.
“This is a bathroom.” An old one at that. Grime was caked into the grout of the black and white tile flooring. Cobwebs fluttered in the corners of the room and a thick layer of dust graced the toilet seats.
“Exactly,” they said, smoothing out the ruffles on their sleeves. “They do not call a toilet a throne for nothing.”
“That’s not—” she shook her head. “Never mind.”
They raised an innocent eyebrow. “Anyways, as I said, I am the ghost of midterms past, but you can call me Omen. Now, do you have your offerings?”
“Offerings make you sound like a demon, you know,” she said, fishing in her bag for the scantrons and fresh pencil. She handed the pack to them.
“And?” They motioned with their hand for more.
“What?”
“My drink!” They huffed impatiently.
“Oh, yeah.” She reached back into her bag and brought out a bottle of caramel iced coffee. “Here.”
“So,” they started, snapping their fingers, a chalkboard with writing appearing next to them, “what service do you need? We have expanded our options recently. I can do ‘deadline extension,’ ‘your teacher made a mistake so the deadline has been extended,’ ‘you caught your professor having an affair, so now they owe you’, though that one can get a bit messy—”

“Okay, I get it,” Darcy interrupted, shaking her head to rid herself of the memory of the last one. “I need to get all As on my midterms.” “For that, there is one condition.” They flipped the chalkboard to the other side. “You have to study. It has to be wholly of your volition. If it is not genuine, your wish will not be granted. Understood?”
She nodded.
The chalkboard disappeared in a comical poof as Omen slipped the scantrons into a folder they hadn’t had before, then placed the iced coffee in a mini fridge that appeared.
Omen slammed the fridge door before it vanished. “I will get your request to headquarters as soon as possible. It was nice doing business with you. Have a wonderful exam season!”
Darcy blinked and they were gone. In fact, she was no longer in the dusty bathroom but was back on the stairs. She glanced at the time on her phone. Only a minute had passed since she had watched her friend head out into the rain.
Crossing her fingers and hoping this worked, Darcy hurried to her next class.


Darcy took a shaky breath, her eyes closed, as she sat in front of her laptop. Rain pounded against her dorm window and thunder clapped overhead adding to her already anxious disposition. She had studied relentlessly. She only hoped it was sincere enough to count. She opened her eyes. All As.
Relief washed over her followed by gratitude. Even though it had been a service she paid for, she wanted to thank Omen, so she grabbed a jacket and rushed out in the rain.
By the time she reached the door to the old stairway, she was dripping wet and panting. Leaving pools of water in her trail , she walked to the thirteenth step and waited for the popping sound.
Things went blurry before shifting back into focus. Omen sat at a wooden desk with teetering stacks of paper on each side, a pair of horn-rimmed glasses they hadn’t worn before perched on the tip of his nose. She was certain they were fake.
“It really worked,” she said, not waiting for him to acknowledge his presence. “What did?” He didn’t bother looking up from his work.
“I got all As on my midterms,” Darcy said. “Your service works.”
He peered over his glasses, a quizzical look on his face. Dark circles under his eyes weighed down his usual chipper attitude and a five o’clock shadow graced his chin. “Headquarters has been down since it flooded earlier this week. No midterm service requests have been fulfilled.”
“Well, surely mine was since I got the grades I wanted.”
With a sigh, Omen took off his glasses and looked Darcy straight in the eye. A small twinkle glinted in his eye. “Or maybe you are a clever person.”
With that, he put the glasses back on upside down and shooed Darcy away with his hand. “Go, I have important matters to attend to.”
Darcy smiled to herself before being taken back to the old staircase.
